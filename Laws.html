Protection of Women from Domestic Violence Act, 2005

Protection of Women from Domestic Violence Act 2005 has come into force from 26.10.2006. The Act is aimed at providing protection to wife or female live-in partner from violence at the hands of the husband or male live-in partner or his relatives. The Law extends protection to women who are sisters including adopted sisters and mothers. Domestic violence under the Act includes actual abuse or threat of abuse, whether physical, sexual, verbal, emotional, or economic. Harassment by way of unlawful dowry demands to the women victim or her relatives would also be covered under the definition of Domestic violence. The Deptt. Of Social Welfare, Government of Delhi is the Nodal Department for the implementation of provisions under the Act. For effective implementation, the Rules provide for Appointment of Protection Officers in each district as per section 8 of the Act and provide them with necessary infrastructure to enable them to start functioning Invite applications from service providers as per Rule 11 and register them after due verification as regards their suitability Publish the names and addresses with telephone number of the Protection Officers and the service providers.

The Sexual Harassment of Women at Workplace (Prevention, Prohibition and Redressal) Act, 2013

Objective: The Act aims to prevent and address incidents of sexual harassment of women at their place of work and provide a mechanism for redressal of complaints related to such harassment.
Key Provisions: The Act provides a clear definition of sexual harassment, encompassing unwelcome acts or behaviour that are sexually colored, creating a hostile or intimidating work environment. It applies to all workplaces, including government, private, and non-governmental organizations. Every workplace employing more than ten persons must constitute an Internal Complaints Committee. A Local Committee is established by the district officer where needed. The Act ensures confidentiality, timely resolution, and protection against retaliation.

The Dowry Prohibition Act, 1961 (Amended in 1986)

Purpose and Scope: The primary goal of the Act is to prohibit the giving or taking of dowry, which is defined as any property or valuable security given or agreed to be given, directly or indirectly, by one party to a marriage to the other, or by the parents of either party, before, during, or at any time after the marriage, in connection with the marriage of the said parties.​
The Act applies to the entirety of India, except for the State of Jammu and Kashmir (as per the original text).
Key Provisions: Giving, taking, or abetting the giving or taking of dowry is punishable with imprisonment, which may extend to five years after the 1986 amendment, and also carries a fine.​
The law excludes presents given at the time of marriage to the bride or bridegroom that are customary and not demanded as dowry, provided a list of such presents and their value is maintained.​
The 1986 amendment made offenses under the Act cognizable, enabling police to arrest without a warrant, and increased penalties for violations.​
Demanding dowry is also a punishable offense under the Act.

The Equal Remuneration Act, 1976

Objective and Scope: The Act was enacted on 11 February 1976 to ensure fair pay for men and women and prevent gender-based discrimination in employment.​
It applies to all establishments across India, in both public and private sectors, employing ten or more persons.​
The law overrides any inconsistent contractual, award, or statutory provisions that conflict with its objectives.
Key Provisions: 1> Equal Pay for Equal Work (Section 4) Employers must pay equal remuneration to men and women workers for the same or similar nature of work. “Similar work” is defined as work requiring similar skill, effort, and responsibility performed under similar working conditions.​
2>No Discrimination in Recruitment (Section 5) Employers cannot discriminate based on gender while recruiting employees for the same kind of work or in matters related to promotion, training, or transfer. Exceptions exist where employment of women in certain hazardous occupations is restricted by law.​
3>Advisory Committee (Section 6) The government may constitute advisory committees to promote employment opportunities for women and improve their working conditions.​
4>Settlement of Claims (Section 7) Authorities appointed under the Act have the power to investigate complaints related to non-payment of equal wages and can order compensation or other remedies. Employees can appeal such orders within 30 days.​
5>Registers and Inspections (Sections 8 & 9) Employers must maintain registers with details of employees, their remuneration, and the work they perform. Inspectors appointed under the Act have powers to inspect records, question employers, and enforce compliance.​
6>Penalties (Section 10) Violation of the Act attracts penalties. Employers may face imprisonment of up to one year or fines up to ₹20,000, or both, for repeated offences.​
7>Overriding Effect (Section 3) The Act supersedes any existing law, award, or contract inconsistent with its provisions, ensuring that the principle of equal pay prevails universally

The Prohibition of Child Marriage Act, 2006

Objective and Scope: The main aim of the Act is to prohibit the solemnization of child marriages and address connected matters.​
It replaces the earlier Child Marriage Restraint Act, 1929, making enforcement stricter and rights more clear for affected children.​
The Act extends to all of India.
Main Provisions: Marriage voidable at minor’s option (Section 3): The party who was a child at the time of marriage can seek annulment by petitioning the district court within 2 years of attaining legal age.​
Maintenance and legitimacy: Orders for maintenance and residence may be made for the female contracting party; children from child marriages are deemed legitimate.​
Void marriages (Section 12): Certain marriages (where the minor is forced, kidnapped, or trafficked) are immediately void, not just voidable.​
Punishments:
Adult male marrying a child: Up to 2 years rigorous imprisonment and/or fine up to ₹1 lakh.​
Persons solemnizing child marriage: Up to 2 years rigorous imprisonment and/or fine up to ₹1 lakh.​
Promoting/abetting/participating (including guardians, relatives, organizers): Up to 2 years rigorous imprisonment and/or fine up to ₹1 lakh. Women are exempt from imprisonment but can be fined.​
Cognizable and non-bailable: All offences under this Act are cognizable and non-bailable.​
Powers of the court: The district court can issue injunctions prohibiting child marriages, and violation of such an order automatically voids the marriage.​
Child Marriage Prohibition Officers: States appoint officers empowered to prevent child marriage, collect evidence, create awareness, and approach courts to seek annulment.

The Indecent Representation of Women (Prohibition) Act, 1986

Objective and Scope: The Act was introduced to prevent the indecent portrayal of women in advertising, publications, writings, paintings, films, and other public representations.​
It extends to the whole of India and came into force on 2 October 1987.​
It aims to safeguard women’s dignity and prevent the misuse of media to exploit or objectify them.
Major Provisions:
Prohibition of Advertisements (Section 3):
No person shall publish, or take part in the publication or exhibition of, any advertisement containing indecent representation of women in any form.​
Prohibition of Publications (Section 4):
Producing, selling, hiring, distributing, circulating, or sending by post any material—such as books, drawings, films, or photographs—containing indecent representations of women is forbidden.​ Exception: Material justified for public good (e.g., in the interest of science, art, or literature), or used bona fide for religious purposes, is exempt.​
Powers of Search and Seizure (Section 5):
Authorized gazetted officers may enter and search premises suspected of containing prohibited material. Seized items can be confiscated and presented as evidence in court.​
Penalties (Section 6):
Offenders may face imprisonment up to 2 years and/or a fine up to ₹2,000 for the first conviction, and up to 5 years imprisonment and a ₹5,000 fine for subsequent convictions.​
Offences by Companies (Section 7):
When the offence is committed by a company, persons in charge of the company, including directors and managers, are deemed guilty unless they prove lack of knowledge or due diligence.​
Protection for Good Faith Acts (Section 9):
No legal proceedings can be taken against anyone acting in good faith under the Act, such as enforcement officials performing their duties.​
Rule-making Powers (Section 10):
The Central Government may prescribe detailed procedures, rules, and enforcement mechanisms under the Act.
